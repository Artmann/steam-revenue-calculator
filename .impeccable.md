## Design Context

### Users
Three audiences share the interface:
- **Indie developers** pricing a game or sizing up similar titles — they want numbers fast and expect the methodology to be explained honestly.
- **Publishers and analysts** looking for ballpark revenue figures across a genre — they need the breakdown table to be skimmable and trustworthy.
- **Press and curious players** who landed here via a blog post — they need enough context to not feel lost, without having the prose dumbed down.

The job to be done is always the same: *"How much did this game actually make?"* — and the answer must come with a credible breakdown (Steam's cut, VAT, refunds, regional pricing, discounts), not just a headline number.

### Brand Personality
- **Voice**: direct, honest, a little wry. The README already sets this tone ("These are estimates, not audits").
- **Three words**: *credible, candid, numerate*.
- **Emotional goal**: the reader should feel they are in the hands of someone who actually ships games and respects their time — **trustworthy and credible**, never salesy.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Editorial / data journalism.** Think Bloomberg, The Pudding, Stripe Press, FT Alphaville. The tool is really a piece of explanatory data journalism that happens to have a calculator in it.

- Confident typography hierarchy — a serif or strong sans display face paired with a refined body face. No Inter. No generic sans as the display voice.
- Generous whitespace, left-aligned prose, asymmetric rather than centered compositions.
- Numbers are the hero of this product. Treat figures like a newspaper treats a pull-quote — tabular lining numerals, clear decimals, currency symbols sized as a subordinate glyph.
- Restrained palette. Warm neutral paper or a dignified near-black, with one editorial accent used sparingly — not a gradient, not neon.
- Data shown in honest tables and small-multiples, not cards-with-sparklines.

**Anti-references (explicitly avoid):**
- Generic SaaS dashboard — card grids, hero metric + gradient CTA, purple-to-blue accents.
- Corporate / enterprise — stock-photo hero, rounded blue buttons, meaningless wrapper prose.
- The current dark theme's default (`#BB86FC` purple on `#222228`) has been chosen without an editorial point of view and should be treated as a starting point to move away from, not a locked palette.

### Design Principles
1. **Numbers are the subject.** Every layout decision should ask "does this make the revenue figure easier to read, trust, and compare?" Typography and spacing serve the numbers, not decorate around them.
2. **Explain honestly.** The Boxleiter method's caveats are a feature, not a disclaimer. Surface the uncertainty — editorial voice over marketing voice.
3. **Rhythm, not repetition.** Tight groupings around the calculator and breakdown, generous separation from the games list and footer. Avoid the uniform-card-grid reflex.
4. **Left-aligned and asymmetric.** Centered layouts read as landing-page template. The interface should feel composed, the way an editorial spread is composed.
5. **Restraint over flourish.** One editorial accent color, one considered display face, one moment of motion per screen. If an effect can't be justified, cut it.
